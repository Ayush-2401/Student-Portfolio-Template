How Abacus Improved My Mental Math Skills

Aarav Sharma
November 12, 2024 · 4 min read

When I first started learning abacus at age 10, I could barely do two-digit multiplication in my head. My teacher would write a problem on the board and I'd reach for my pencil while other students were already shouting answers. Fast forward three years, and I can calculate five-digit additions in under two seconds. The abacus did that for me.

It sounds impossible, right? That a wooden frame with beads sliding on rods could transform how your brain processes numbers. But after two years in the GEMMA Abacus Club and earning my Level 5 certification, I can tell you from experience: it absolutely works — and the science backs it up.

What Actually Changes in Your Brain

When you learn abacus, you are not just memorising positions of beads. You are building a mental image — what abacus masters call a "mental abacus." After months of practice, I began to close my eyes and visualise the beads moving in my head. My fingers would twitch slightly as I calculated. I was doing the same physical motion, just mentally.

"The abacus does not replace thinking — it upgrades the hardware your thinking runs on."

Research shows that abacus training activates both hemispheres of the brain — the left (logical) and right (visual-spatial). Most people only use the left hemisphere for math. Abacus students use both, creating stronger neural connections for numerical processing. The result: faster, more accurate mental calculation without pen and paper.

How I Prepared for the International Olympiad

The International Abacus Olympiad is not a casual competition. Students from over 30 countries compete across multiple rounds of mental arithmetic — additions, subtractions, multiplications, and mixed problems — with decreasing time limits at each level.

My preparation involved three phases. First, I drilled foundational speed — 500 problems per day, aiming to reduce my average response time by 10ms each week. Second, I practised accuracy under pressure using timed tests with distractions. Third, I visualised — every night for 20 minutes, I would close my eyes and run through problem sets using only my mental abacus.

When I stood in the competition hall and the timer started, I felt completely calm. Not because I wasn't nervous — I absolutely was — but because my brain had done this thousands of times before. When the final round ended and my score was announced as Gold, I almost didn't believe it. Then the medal was placed around my neck, and I burst into tears. The good kind.

Benefits Beyond Mathematics

The unexpected benefit of abacus training has been in every other subject. My focus during exams improved dramatically. I found I could hold more information in working memory — useful when solving multi-step physics problems. Even my reading comprehension improved because I could track more details simultaneously.

If you are a student wondering whether abacus is worth the effort: it absolutely is. Start slow, stay consistent, and trust the process. The mental abacus you build will serve you for life — not just in mathematics, but in everything that requires sharp, focused thinking.

And if you ever compete internationally, remember: the thousands of boring practice sessions are the actual gold medal. The ceremony is just the celebration.
