How Abacus Improved My Mental Math Skills

When I first started learning abacus at age 10, I could barely do two-digit multiplication in my head. The beads looked confusing, the movements seemed impossible to memorise. But my instructor at the GEMMA Abacus Club had a saying: "Trust the beads, they will trust you back."

AS
Aarav Sharma
Grade 8 · GEMMA Student
Read Full Post →

Three years later, I won a Gold Medal at the International Abacus Olympiad — and I can multiply four-digit numbers in my head faster than most adults can reach for a calculator. Here's how that transformation happened.


The abacus is more than a counting tool. It's a mental gymnasium. When you move the beads, you're not just calculating — you're training your brain to visualise numbers as physical objects. Over time, the physical abacus disappears, and you carry an invisible one in your mind. This is called the "mental abacus" technique, and it's what elite abacus students use in competitions.


My training involved 30 minutes of daily practice — adding columns of 10 numbers, multiplying, dividing, all on the beads. It was repetitive, sometimes boring, but the results were undeniable. By the end of my first year, my mental arithmetic speed had doubled. By the end of year two, I was competing at the state level.


The most surprising benefit wasn't speed — it was confidence. Knowing I could solve complex calculations instantly made me fearless in mathematics class. I stopped dreading exams and started enjoying them. My class rank improved significantly, and I began helping classmates understand concepts that had once confused me.


For students considering abacus training: start early, be patient, and trust the process. The beads won't let you down. And one day, when you're standing on a stage with a gold medal around your neck, you'll understand exactly why it was worth every single practice session.

My Experience at the International Student Speaker Competition

Standing on stage in front of 200 people from 14 countries, I realised that preparation is everything. My hands were trembling, my heart was racing — but the moment I spoke my first sentence, something clicked.

AS
Aarav Sharma
Grade 8 · GEMMA Student
Read Full Post →

I had spent three months preparing for this competition. My topic was climate change — a subject I had studied deeply through my Science Club and the Climate Change Awareness Website I had built. But knowing the content and delivering it confidently are two very different skills.


My preparation involved writing and rewriting my speech seven times. Each draft was cleaner, more impactful. My Public Speaking Club mentor, Mrs. Kapoor, pushed me to stop memorising words and start owning the message. "Speak from what you feel, not what you've written," she told me. That advice changed everything.


On competition day, I arrived early and watched the other participants rehearse. Students from the UK, Australia, Singapore, USA — all confident, polished, with years of drama training behind them. I felt like an imposter. But then I remembered: I had something they didn't. I had lived through Delhi's pollution, I had seen the Yamuna river shrink year after year. My speech was not just information — it was personal.


When I walked onto the stage, I paused for three seconds before speaking — just as I had practised. That pause centred me. I delivered my six-minute speech on climate change and its impact on India's agricultural communities, using three stories I had personally researched.


The Bronze Medal was not what I expected. But the experience of standing on that international stage, representing my school and my country, was worth a thousand gold medals. I came home not with just a certificate, but with a voice — and the confidence to use it.

Coding · January 20, 2024 · 4 min read

Why Every Student Should Learn to Code

My coding journey started with Scratch when I was 11. I wanted to make a game — and that simple desire changed how I think about everything. Learning to code is one of the most valuable skills for future careers in technology, engineering, and innovation.

AS
Aarav Sharma
Grade 8 · GEMMA Student
Read Full Post →

I remember staring at the Scratch editor for the first time, completely overwhelmed. There were so many blocks — motion, sound, events, control. Where do you even begin? I clicked "when green flag clicked" and dragged a "move 10 steps" block underneath it. The sprite moved. And just like that, I was hooked.


Coding teaches you something that no other subject does: how to break a complex problem into tiny, solvable steps. When my game character kept falling through the floor, I didn't panic — I debugged. I tested one thing at a time, methodically, until I found the collision detection error. This kind of systematic thinking has transformed how I approach every problem, from mathematics proofs to science experiments.


But the benefits go far deeper than problem-solving. Coding gives you agency — the power to build things that didn't exist before. When I completed Temple Adventure and published it on Scratch, and then saw "200 plays" next to my name, I felt a pride that no exam mark had ever given me. Real people, from real places, were playing something I had created. That feeling is irreplaceable.


For students who think coding is too hard or "only for computer people" — please, give it one week with Scratch. It's free, it's visual, and it's designed for learners. You won't become a software engineer overnight, but you will start seeing the world differently. You'll look at apps and websites and think, "How did they build that?" — and then you'll realise you can find out.


In a world increasingly driven by technology, coding is not just a skill — it's a language. And every student deserves to speak it.
