My Experience at the International Student Speaker Competition

Aarav Sharma
September 3, 2023 · 5 min read

Standing on stage in front of 200 people from 14 countries, I realised that preparation is everything. The nerves were there — they always are — but underneath them was something else: a quiet confidence built from months of practice, coaching, and one very memorable stage fright incident in the school corridor that I'd rather forget.

The International Student Speaker Competition is one of the most prestigious public speaking events for students aged 10–15. In 2023, I represented my school and the GEMMA Public Speaking Club for the first time. I had no idea what to expect. What I got was one of the most transformative experiences of my life.

How It All Started

I joined the GEMMA Public Speaking Club after Mrs. Priya Nair, my class teacher, spotted me giving an impromptu answer in science class and said, "Aarav, you should do this on a stage." I was terrified. Speaking in class to 35 students was already stressful — speaking to hundreds was unimaginable.

But I took the plunge. Mr. Anil Mehta, the club's coach, worked with me for six months on structure, delivery, vocal variety, and handling silences. We practised in front of mirrors, on video, and eventually in front of small audiences of parents and students. Each session was humbling and motivating at the same time.

My Speech: Small Footprints, Big Futures

My topic was climate change — specifically, what young people can do right now, not in 20 years. I called the speech "Small Footprints, Big Futures." I opened with a statistic: that a child born in 2011 (my birth year) will face twice the number of extreme weather events in their lifetime compared to a person born in 1960.

"We are not the problem. We are the urgency. And we are also the solution."

Writing the speech took three weeks and nine drafts. My father read every single one and gave honest feedback. My mother timed me with a stopwatch. My younger sister was my most honest critic: "It's boring there," she'd say, pointing at paragraph four. She was usually right.

The Competition Day

We arrived at the venue the evening before. Meeting students from Singapore, the UK, Nigeria, and Malaysia was incredible. Everyone was nervous but also enthusiastic. We shared tips, laughed about our preparation stories, and made friends in those 24 hours that I still keep in touch with today.

When my number was called and I walked to the podium, time slowed down. I looked at the audience, took one breath, and began. The speech flowed. The silence I had been afraid of became powerful pauses. The eye contact I'd practised felt natural. At the end, there was genuine applause — not polite clapping, but real appreciation from an international audience.

I won the Bronze Medal. Was I disappointed not to win Gold? Honestly, for about five minutes, yes. Then I realised what I had actually won: the ability to speak on an international stage and hold an audience's attention. That skill is worth far more than any medal. The medal is the proof. The skill is the prize.

Lessons That Will Last a Lifetime

Three things I learned from this experience: First, preparation is not about memorising your speech — it is about understanding it so deeply that you could speak it a hundred different ways. Second, nerves are fuel. Channel them, do not suppress them. Third, every audience wants you to succeed. They are on your side from the first word.

If you are a student who is afraid of public speaking — I understand completely. I was you. But take the first step. Join a club, speak at a class assembly, say something at dinner. Every word spoken in public is practice for the stage. And the stage, when you finally reach it, is extraordinary.
